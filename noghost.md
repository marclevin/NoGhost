# Two Walls: Stopping Insider Fraud in Prepaid Electricity Tokens

## Introduction

Prepaid electricity in South Africa loses somewhere between R23 billion and R45 billion a year to a fraud called "ghost vending" — insiders generating valid electricity tokens without anyone paying for them. This is not an outside hacking problem; it is a structural one. The two facts that should always go together — *a payment happened* and *a token was generated* — currently live in one database controlled by the very organisation whose insiders commit the fraud.

This document sets out a system that splits those two facts across **two independent walls**: a bank rail that proves the money is real, and a threshold-cryptography consortium that proves the token is real. No single party — not a merchant, not an insider — can breach both. The blockchain's role is deliberately narrow: coordinate the parties, record every authorisation immutably, and govern who the trusted parties are.

---

## Who experiences this problem

**The utility (e.g. Eskom) and municipalities** lose the money directly — billions a year in electricity consumed but never paid for.

**Paying customers** carry it indirectly. Lost revenue is recovered through higher tariffs and municipal debt that lands on ratepayers. Honest customers end up subsidising the fraud.

**The state and honest oversight** experience it as something that cannot properly be investigated, because the records anyone would need are held by the same organisation whose insiders are committing the fraud.

This is not unique to South Africa. Any country that issues prepaid utility tokens from a central system — electricity, water, prepaid gas — has the same structural weakness.

## What the problem actually is

Prepaid electricity works through a **token**: a 20-digit number the customer types into their meter. The number is computed from that specific meter's secret key plus the amount purchased. Enter a valid number and the meter releases that much electricity.

"Ghost vending" is when someone with legitimate access **generates valid tokens with no payment behind them** — insiders who hold the key material, producing real working tokens from a desk and selling them, while the utility collects nothing.

The root cause, stated plainly:

> **"A token was generated" and "a payment was received" live in the same database, controlled by the same organisation whose insiders are the ones committing the fraud.**

Because one party controls both facts, a dishonest insider can create the first without the second ever existing — and make the records look consistent. Existing fixes (recoding meters, smarter meter hardware) make tokens harder to *forge from outside*. They do nothing about *authorised insiders* generating from inside. That is the gap.

## What the solution is

Split the two facts that should never be separable across **two independent walls**, so that no single party controls both — and so that the act of creating a token cannot happen without independent parties agreeing.

> **The bank guarantees the money is real. The consortium guarantees the token is real. The merchant only asks — and pays to ask.**

**Wall 1 — the payment is proven by the bank, not by anyone's word.**
The merchant sends a request to generate a token. Generating it debits the merchant's own bank account, and the bank confirms that debit. There is no "payment claim" for anyone to lie about — the money moved or it didn't, and the bank is the witness on every single transaction. However the *customer* paid the merchant (cash, card, mobile money) is the merchant's own business; the leg the system cares about is always a real, bank-witnessed debit.

**Wall 2 — the token can only be generated by an agreeing group, never one party.**
Producing a token requires several independent parties (the utility and the municipalities/vendors who collect from consumers) to jointly perform the cryptographic operation. Every one of them is a party that *loses money to the fraud* — there is no separate "auditor" seat, because the parties who benefit from stopping the fraud are exactly the ones holding the shares. No single party — even one *inside* the consortium — can generate a token alone. The master key is never held in one place by anyone (explained below). One insider is powerless; fraud would require collusion across organisations that do not answer to the same boss.

**Every authorisation is recorded permanently**, where no single party can alter it — so any token that appears at a meter *without* a matching record is provably illegitimate, and attributable.

The honest boundary of the claim: this kills the **systemic** fraud — the "generate billions from a desk" attack that is the actual multi-billion-rand problem. It does not stop someone physically ripping the key out of a single meter's hardware, but that yields one meter's worth of tokens, not a national operation. Scoping to the systemic attack is exactly right.

## Blockchain specifics

### The two roles — keep this wall clean

The single most important structural point:

- **Merchant = requester.** They send a generation request and their bank account is debited for it. They are vetted and revocable (they are not bonded — the per-request bank debit is what holds them honest, see below) — but they **never touch the key material** and take **no part** in generating the token.
- **Consortium (the trusted validator set) = generators.** They hold the key shares and jointly produce the token. This is the *only* set of parties involved in generation.

The merchant asks; the consortium generates. Nothing crosses that line.

### The end-to-end flow

1. **Customer pays the merchant** by any method — this leg is the merchant's own concern.
2. **Merchant submits a generation request:** "token for meter #123, 40 kWh."
3. **Merchant's bank account is debited**, and the bank confirms the debit. This confirmation — produced by the bank, not the merchant — is the proof of payment.
4. **The consortium sees the confirmed debit and the request** (confirm-*then*-generate: nothing is produced until the debit is confirmed).
5. **The consortium runs the threshold ceremony** and produces the STS token.
6. **The token is returned to the customer**, and the authorisation is recorded immutably.

The merchant appears only in steps 2–3. Generation is steps 4–5, and it is *only* the consortium.

### Where the blockchain actually fits (and where it doesn't)

Be precise, because this is where most "blockchain fixes X" pitches overreach:

- The blockchain **does not** physically stop a number from being computed. A token is just math on a key; no ledger can reach into that.
- The **prevention** comes from cryptography (below), not from the ledger.
- The **blockchain's real jobs** are: coordinate the multi-party approval, hold the permanent tamper-proof record of every authorisation, and govern who the trusted parties are.

In one line: **cryptography prevents the fraud; the blockchain coordinates the parties, records the act immutably, and governs the consortium.**

### The cryptographic core: threshold signing (so no one ever holds the key)

This is what turns the system from "detects fraud" into "prevents fraud."

- The master signing key is created in **shares** through a distributed ceremony. Each consortium party holds **one share**. The full key is **never assembled anywhere — not even for an instant, not even during setup**.
- To generate a token, each shareholder performs a **partial computation using only their share**. The partials combine into a valid token **without the full key ever being reconstructed**.
- So generation is not gated by a permission check that could be bypassed — it is gated by **mathematics**. The token cannot be computed unless the required number of independent parties each take part. An insider with one share holds a useless fragment, and there is no moment where an assembled key sits in some machine's memory to be stolen.

The general primitive is **threshold cryptography**, built on multi-party computation. Note this is **not** the same as a smart-contract multisig: multisig *counts approvals* for an on-chain action; threshold cryptography *jointly produces one output* under one key that was never assembled. It must happen **off-chain**, because anything inside a smart contract is re-executed by every node and therefore has no secrets — a private key in a contract is a published key.

One honest technical caveat about *which* primitive. A real STS meter does not verify a signature; it **symmetrically decrypts** a token derived from a master vending key. So the faithful production primitive is **secure MPC over that single symmetric key** — the key split into shares, the token jointly computed, the full key never assembled — which is harder than, and different from, a threshold *signature* scheme.

*(For the prototype we use a threshold **signature** scheme — FROST (Ed25519) — as an honest, working stand-in. It genuinely delivers the one property that carries the whole argument: a single key, split across parties, that no one party can use alone. The specific algorithm is a simplification; the "no single holder" property is real.)*

### On-chain vs off-chain — one consistent principle

The chain is used only where being public, tamper-proof, and consensus-checked *adds* something. Secrets stay off it.

- **On-chain:** the generation request, the confirmed-debit reference, the consortium's authorisation, and the permanent audit record. No customer payment details and no personal data (respecting privacy law such as POPIA) — typically a hash plus the minimal fields needed to authorise and later reconcile.
- **Off-chain:** the actual bank settlement, and the threshold-signing ceremony itself (the key shares never go near the ledger).

The same principle runs through the whole design: **payments stay on their existing rails, key operations stay in the parties' own hands — the chain coordinates and witnesses, it does not replace either.**

### The consensus / trust model

This is a **consortium**, not a public free-for-all — a small set of known, accountable institutions (the utility and the municipalities/vendors who collect from consumers). That is the honest shape of the problem: you do not need thousands of anonymous nodes, you need a handful of named parties who all *lose money to the fraud* and who do not all answer to the same boss.

A useful consequence: **the validators do not need to be paid to participate.** Their incentive is intrinsic — they are the ones losing billions to fraud, so reduced fraud *is* the reward. On validator-list chains such as XRPL or Stellar, this maps naturally: validators are trusted by being listed, not by earning a block subsidy.

Merchants are not bonded due to the need of their bank account to be debited for every request. The consortium is bonded because they hold the key shares and are accountable for the integrity of the generation process. The only issue is spam, which can be handled by a small per-request fee (e.g. 1 cent) to prevent denial-of-service attacks, which is trivial compared to the billions at stake.

### Where the economics lives

Not in "paying validators." The economics is the accountability layer and the conservation property:

- **Consortium bonding / slashing** guards *generation integrity* — if a member's share is used to co-sign a fraudulent generation, that member's bond is slashed. This turns insider collusion from a free option into a punishable, expensive one, which no existing anti-fraud measure does.
- **Conservation:** every token now maps to a real, bank-witnessed debit. "Tokens generated = money received" becomes a hard settlement fact rather than an accounting convention you have to trust — arguably the cleanest economic story of all, because the value is real bank money, not an invented internal token.

### Chain choice

The problem is payment-shaped and consortium-shaped, so the natural candidates are:

- **XRPL** — trusted-validator (UNL) model, fast finality, native primitives for conditional actions and multi-sign; strong if validator infrastructure already exists.
- **Stellar** — same family (federated agreement, choose-your-validators), explicitly aimed at developing-economy financial infrastructure; an equally clean fit.
- **Hyperledger Fabric / Corda** — the textbook *production* answer for a known-institution consortium: permissioned, fine-grained approval policies, inter-party data privacy, no native coin to design around. The cost is heavier setup.

A reasonable position for a prototype: **build on XRPL or Stellar for speed and native primitives, and name Fabric/Corda as the likely production architecture.** Stating that distinction shows the difference between a quick prototype and the correct production design.

### The scope boundary, stated honestly

- **Payment fraud:** closed. Every token is backed by a real, bank-witnessed debit; there is no payment claim to fake.
- **Systemic generation fraud:** closed. No token exists without threshold agreement across independent parties, and the master key is never assembled anywhere.
- **Out of scope:** a meter whose individual key is physically extracted from the hardware. That is a per-meter physical attack yielding one meter's tokens — not the systemic, generate-from-a-desk fraud this design eliminates.
- **Settlement reversals** (a debit later disputed) are handled by ordinary payment-dispute processes, as in any payment system, and are not special to this design.

Naming these boundaries is a strength, not a weakness — it shows the claim is scoped to exactly what the design delivers.
